First Visit Experience: What Actually Happens

February 15, 2025 8 min read By Sarah Mitchell
Source: Unsplash

Walking into Dubai Pool for the first time, I had expectations shaped by Instagram photos and travel blogs. Reality, as it often does, had different plans. The check-in process, the layout, the actual vibe versus what's marketed—here's the unfiltered breakdown of that initial visit and what I wish I'd known beforehand.

The Arrival and Check-In Process

I arrived around 11 AM on a Thursday, thinking mid-week would mean fewer crowds. The entrance is more understated than I expected—no grand archway or obvious signage from the street. Once you find it, there's a reception area that feels more like a hotel lobby than a pool facility.

The check-in took about five minutes. They asked for ID, confirmed my booking (I'd reserved online the night before), and handed me a wristband and a towel. The wristband system is straightforward—you need it to access different areas. What I didn't realize is that you also need it to order food and drinks, which became important later.

One thing that caught me off guard: they have a strict bag check policy. They're looking for outside food and drinks, which aren't allowed. I had a water bottle that was fine, but I saw someone ahead of me having to leave their snacks at reception. Good to know if you're planning to bring your own food.

Source: Unsplash

The Layout: Finding Your Way Around

The facility is larger than it appears from the outside. There are multiple pool areas—a main pool that's quite spacious, a smaller relaxation pool that's warmer, and what they call the "infinity edge" section that overlooks the city. The layout isn't immediately intuitive, and I spent the first twenty minutes just figuring out where everything was.

The changing rooms are located on the ground floor, near the entrance. They're clean and well-maintained, with individual cubicles, showers, and lockers. The lockers use a key system—you get a key when you check in, and it's attached to your wristband. I found the lockers to be a decent size, enough for a day bag and a change of clothes.

What I appreciated: the changing rooms have hairdryers and basic toiletries. What I didn't love: the floor gets quite wet, and the non-slip mats aren't as effective as they could be. I'd recommend bringing flip-flops for the changing area.

The Pool Areas: First Impressions

The main pool is impressive—it's long enough for actual swimming, not just lounging. The water temperature was perfect, not too cold, not too warm. The depth varies, with a shallow end for kids and families, and a deeper section that's probably around 1.8 meters.

What surprised me was the crowd. Even on a Thursday, it was busier than I expected. The poolside loungers fill up quickly, especially the ones in the shade. By noon, most of the prime spots were taken. If you want a specific location, arrive early or be prepared to wait.

The infinity edge section is smaller but offers the best views. It's also where most people want to take photos, so it can get crowded. The water there is slightly cooler, which I actually preferred after being in the main pool for a while.

Source: Unsplash

The Food and Drink Situation

There's a restaurant and a poolside bar. I tried the restaurant for lunch around 1 PM. The menu is international with some Middle Eastern options. Prices are what you'd expect at a premium facility—not outrageous, but definitely not budget-friendly. A main course runs around 80-120 AED, and drinks are 25-45 AED.

The food quality was good, not exceptional. I had a grilled chicken salad that was fresh and well-prepared. The service was attentive, though it took a while to get attention during peak hours. They bring the food to your lounger if you prefer, which is convenient.

One thing to note: you can't pay with cash at the poolside. Everything goes through your wristband, and you settle up when you leave. This is efficient, but it also means it's easy to lose track of spending. I ended up spending more than I planned because I wasn't keeping mental tabs.

What I Wish I'd Known

  • Arrive early: The best spots are gone by 11:30 AM, even on weekdays.
  • Bring sunscreen: They sell it, but it's expensive. The sun is intense, and shade is limited.
  • Water shoes help: The pool deck gets hot, and the changing room floors are wet.
  • Book in advance: While walk-ins are sometimes possible, booking guarantees entry, especially on weekends.
  • Check the weather: They close during sandstorms, which happen more often than you'd think.

The Reality Versus Expectations

Social media shows Dubai Pool as this serene, almost empty paradise. The reality is that it's a popular spot, and it gets crowded. The facilities are nice, but they're not five-star luxury—more like a well-maintained premium facility. The staff are professional and helpful, but they're also managing a lot of people, so don't expect personalized attention.

What exceeded my expectations: the water quality (crystal clear), the views from the infinity section, and the overall cleanliness of the facility. What fell short: the crowd management (it can feel chaotic during peak times), the food prices relative to quality, and the limited shade options.

Would I Go Back?

Yes, but with adjusted expectations. It's a nice place to spend a day, especially if you're looking for a pool experience in the city. It's not a hidden gem or a secret spot—it's a well-known facility that delivers a solid experience if you know what you're getting into.

For a first visit, I'd recommend going on a weekday, arriving early, and being prepared for crowds. Bring what you need (sunscreen, water shoes, a good book), and budget more than you think you'll spend. Most importantly, don't expect the Instagram-perfect empty pool—those photos are either from very early morning or heavily edited.

If you're planning your first visit, I hope this helps set realistic expectations. It's a good experience, just not the flawless paradise that marketing materials sometimes suggest.
